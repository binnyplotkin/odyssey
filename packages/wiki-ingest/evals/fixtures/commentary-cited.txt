Reading the Call of Abram: Notes on Genesis 12

The narrative of Genesis 12 opens with an imperative whose abruptness has troubled readers for centuries. "Go forth from your land" arrives with no introduction of the man addressed — Abram has not spoken, has done nothing to merit election, and receives no explanation. Alter renders the phrase "lekh lekha" as "go forth" and notes in his commentary that the Hebrew doubling carries an intensive force lost in most translations, something closer to "take yourself off." [1]

Medieval commentary heard the same abruptness and answered it with biography. Rashi, drawing on midrashic tradition, supplies the missing backstory: Abram had already recognized his Creator amid an idolatrous generation, and the command therefore comes to a man already turned toward God. [2] The plain-sense reading resists this harmonization. Speiser insists that the Yahwist author presents the call as unmotivated precisely because the initiative belongs wholly to the deity — a theological point, not a biographical gap. [3]

The promise attached to the command is threefold: land, offspring, and a great name. Sarna observes that each element answers a corresponding lack announced at the end of Genesis 11 — Sarai's barrenness, the family's interrupted migration, the scattering at Babel. [4] The literary architecture is deliberate: chapter 11 poses the problems that chapter 12 resolves in promise.

The famine episode that closes the chapter sits uneasily beside the promise. No sooner has Abram built altars in the land than he abandons it for Egypt, and there passes his wife off as his sister. Von Rad reads the wife-sister episode as deliberately scandalous: the bearer of promise endangers the promise in the very act of preserving himself, and only divine intervention — the plagues on Pharaoh's house — rescues the future from Abram's own stratagem. [5] The narrator offers no moral commentary, which Alter takes as characteristic of biblical narrative art: judgment is enacted through consequence, not editorializing. [1]

What emerges from these readings is a chapter about promise sustained against its bearer. The election is unexplained, the elect is fallible, and the narrative repeatedly stages the gap between the two.
